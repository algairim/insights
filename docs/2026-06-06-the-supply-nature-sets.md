# When Nature Sets the Supply and You Plan by Feel

`2026 June 6`

A particular kind of business runs on a clock it does not control. The [harvest window for a fruit that bruises](disclaimer.md) is six weeks. [Thirty peach varieties ripen in sequence](disclaimer.md), and the crowd arrives by weather, not by booking. [One salt harvest in August has to be sold for the next twelve months](disclaimer.md). [One bloom a year is sold four ways](disclaimer.md). The supply is fixed by nature; the demand is largely known by the calendar. And yet the plan that connects them is almost always made by feel.

This is not a small inefficiency. A [seasonal producer working from last year's guess](disclaimer.md) either runs short of its best line in the week that matters or sits on stock it made too much of. The [study of demand forecasting](disclaimer.md) describes exactly the tool for this: producing and ordering against a model of likely demand rather than against memory and instinct. The data these businesses need already sits in their own past seasons.

The [seasonal intelligence](disclaimer.md) concept frames the opportunity well: the analysis that matters is the one done before the next season opens, not the post-mortem after it closes. For a business whose entire year turns on a few fixed windows, the difference between planning by feel and planning by its own history is the difference between selling out at the right price and discounting what nature already decided it could only make once.
