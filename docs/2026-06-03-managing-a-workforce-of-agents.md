# Your next hire might be an agent. Someone still has to manage it.

`2026 June 3`

The software the largest vendors are now shipping assumes something the average business has not yet absorbed: that AI agents will sit alongside people in the same workflows, drawing on the same data, doing actual work rather than suggesting it. One major networking vendor has shipped a product that [puts humans and AI agents on a single data layer](disclaimer.md). A leading enterprise platform has [embedded an agent coworker in every search bar](disclaimer.md). Another shipped [a management layer explicitly for fleets of agents](disclaimer.md). And a major HR-and-finance platform reported [200% growth in agentic deployments while quietly flagging the paradox](disclaimer.md): the agents work, and nobody has decided who manages them.

That is the gap. An agent that takes actions needs supervision, permissions, and accountability the same way a new employee does, and the [ways multi-agent systems fail in deployment](disclaimer.md) are mostly failures of orchestration, not intelligence. The deeper unease is named by the [loyalty of a tool](disclaimer.md) concept: an agent that acts as you, on your behalf, can serve a master you cannot see, and trusting its claimed identity without verifying it is how the quiet failures begin.

The work that is about to be scarce is not building agents. It is managing them. The org chart is gaining members who never clock off, and almost no business has written down who is responsible when one of them gets it wrong.
