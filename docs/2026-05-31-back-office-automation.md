# The Back-Office Problem

`2026 May 31`

Customer service was the first domain AI automated, followed by sales and marketing. The back office — accounts payable, procurement reconciliation, compliance attestations, vendor onboarding, contract review — held out because it is where exceptions live. The 95% of transactions that flow through a deterministic workflow were already automated by conventional RPA years ago. The 5% that do not — the invoice in an unusual format, the contract with a non-standard clause, the compliance check requiring evidence from three systems — are exactly what occupies the back-office team's day. [Salesforce's Agentforce Operations](disclaimer.md) and [ServiceNow's autonomous workforce expansion](disclaimer.md) both target this exception path specifically and report 70–80% cycle-time reductions for the tasks they successfully scope.

The pattern holds in healthcare. [US hospitals jumped from 3% to 22% AI use](disclaimer.md) and the value landed in back-office first — scheduling, prior authorisation, documentation. [NHS SBS deployed Salesforce Agentforce](disclaimer.md) and cut 12-minute call-handling to 3. [South West London NHS signed Lyrebird for 20,000 clinicians](disclaimer.md) for ambient clinical documentation. The administrative burden that surrounds the core service — the paperwork, the routing, the exception handling — is the first to move and the easiest to measure, because it produces a clear before and after.

For SMEs the pattern is directly relevant and structurally harder to act on. Case studies across sectors — from a [letting agency](disclaimer.md) to a [language academy](disclaimer.md) to a [honey producer](disclaimer.md) — surface the same structural finding: the most valuable process to automate is the administrative spine that surrounds the actual business, not the core craft. Quoting, scheduling, compliance tracking, stock reconciliation — these are not glamorous. They are where a measurable, fast-returning pilot almost always lives.
