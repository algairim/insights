# AI Hallucination Risk

`2026 May 31`

AI hallucination — the confident generation of false information — is the most widely discussed AI failure mode and the most misunderstood in its practical implications. The risk is not that models are generally unreliable. It is that they are unreliable in unpredictable ways, producing fluent, confident, plausible-sounding output that is factually wrong without any visible signal of uncertainty. [South Africa withdrew its Draft National AI Policy](disclaimer.md) after it was found to contain hallucinated citations — AI-generated references to academic papers that do not exist. [Mosaic and Munich Re launched a $15 million AI hallucination insurance product](disclaimer.md): simultaneously a sign that the risk is real enough to be insured and that the insurance industry has now priced it. [Canada's privacy commissioners ruled against OpenAI under PIPEDA](disclaimer.md) specifically for AI-generated false information about real individuals.

The practical response is not to avoid AI but to design workflows that do not require infallibility. Tasks where hallucination can be caught — draft generation, summarisation, first-pass analysis — are low-risk because a human reviews the output. Tasks where hallucination is invisible — automated client communications, compliance documentation, legal drafting submitted without review — are high-risk. The governance question for any AI deployment is: for every AI-assisted output that leaves the building, is there a human who has read it and is accountable for it? [Human-in-the-loop design](disclaimer.md) is not a workaround for a failing technology. It is the correct design pattern for a technology that is powerful but not infallible. The businesses deploying AI effectively in 2026 treat it as a high-capability collaborator, not an autonomous authority.
