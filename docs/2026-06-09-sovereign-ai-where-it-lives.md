`2026 June 9`

For most of the last two years the question was which model to use. A quieter question is now arriving underneath it: where does the model run, and who can turn it off. Governments have noticed that the intelligence their economies are starting to depend on sits on a handful of foreign servers, and they are spending to change that. Spain has paired [Indra and Cohere to build sovereign AI for Spanish firms in five languages](disclaimer.md); the [UAE has turned sovereign AI from slogan into a platform that firms onboard to](disclaimer.md) and is putting [the agentic workforce on infrastructure that cannot leave the country](disclaimer.md); [Morocco landed Africa's first sovereign AI platform](disclaimer.md); and [Chile shipped an open Spanish-Portuguese model built for $550k](disclaimer.md). Europe's own [tech-sovereignty package is quietly lowering the AI Act bar for small firms](disclaimer.md) while it builds the alternative.

For a business this is less abstract than it sounds. Every prompt a firm sends carries data somewhere, and [where those prompts actually travel is a question with a legal answer](disclaimer.md). The sovereignty movement is widening the menu: you can now keep inference on data the cloud provider [cannot read at all](disclaimer.md), or run [open-weight models you host yourself](disclaimer.md) rather than renting access to a black box you do not control.

The strategic point is small and durable. A capability you cannot relocate is a capability someone else governs. The firms thinking about this now are not being paranoid — they are keeping the exit open before they need it.
