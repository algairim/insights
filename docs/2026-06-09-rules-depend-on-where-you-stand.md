`2026 June 9`

There is no single rulebook for AI, and there is not going to be one. The same system that is lightly regulated in one jurisdiction is high-risk and constrained in another, and the gap is widening rather than closing. In Europe, [enforcement powers for general-purpose AI go live in August](disclaimer.md) and Brussels has [mapped which uses count as high-risk](disclaimer.md), with [Spain passing its own organic law on top, including a deepfake ban](disclaimer.md). In the United States the trajectory points the other way: the White House has [bet on frontier AI that is voluntary and unlicensed](disclaimer.md), and Colorado [gutted America's first state AI law weeks before it bound anyone](disclaimer.md).

For a firm operating in one country this looks like someone else's problem. It stops looking that way the moment a customer, a supplier, or a hosted model sits in another. A tool that is fine to deploy at home can trigger obligations the instant its output reaches a regulated market. Knowing [what the EU AI Act actually requires and from whom](disclaimer.md), and [what GDPR demands once your systems process data through a model](disclaimer.md), is no longer a compliance footnote — it is part of deciding where the business can go next.

The deeper shift is that geography has become a feature of the technology. The same answer, the same model, the same automation: legal here, restricted there. And the map keeps moving.
