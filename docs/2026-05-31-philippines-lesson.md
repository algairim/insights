# The Philippines Lesson

`2026 May 31`

The number that circled Manila's economic debate for months became concrete in February 2026: [more than a quarter of Philippine employment — roughly 12.7 million jobs — is exposed to generative AI](disclaimer.md). The exposure concentrates precisely where the country built its modern prosperity. The IT-BPO sector — call centres, customer support, back-office processing — employs around two million people and generates revenue equivalent to 8% of GDP. It is structured, language-heavy, repeatable work. For two decades the Philippines sold the world its capacity to do exactly the tasks that AI has now learned to do.

What makes the Philippine case instructive is the response. The industry body still projects $42 billion in revenue and close to two million jobs for 2026, but the composition of those jobs is shifting underneath the headline stability. The sector added around 80,000 higher-value roles in data science, AI oversight, and complex problem resolution while an estimated 3% of the workforce sits in roles at high displacement risk. A government-backed retraining programme, Project UNLAD, is converting traditional agents into AI-fluent supervisory roles, and reskilled workers are reportedly seeing pay rises averaging 25%. The defining image of 2026 in Manila is the retirement of the "call centre agent" in favour of the "AI pilot" — one person supervising a fleet of five to ten agentic AI instances.

The lesson that travels beyond the Philippines is that work changed altitude, not disappeared. The tasks that vanished were narrow and repeatable. The roles that appeared are supervisory: pointing the AI, checking it, handling the exceptions it cannot. [The AI readiness literature](disclaimer.md) makes the same observation at the SME level: the most experienced person in any small business typically spends their day on work an agent could now handle. The organisation that asks its experienced people to become pilots — retaining them and paying more for the supervisory skill — ends up ahead. The organisation that simply removes the role loses both the efficiency and the knowledge in the same move.
