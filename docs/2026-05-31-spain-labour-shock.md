# Spain's Labour Shock

`2026 May 31`

[Funcas, Spain's leading economic research centre, published in May 2026](disclaimer.md) the first Spanish-specific quantification of AI's labour-market impact. The headline number — 3.5 million jobs at risk — was the one that travelled. The number that matters is net: in the moderate scenario, between 1.7 and 2.3 million jobs disappear over the next decade against roughly 1.6 million created, leaving a net loss of approximately 600,000 positions. There is no Spanish scenario in the Funcas model where AI net-adds jobs over the coming decade.

The asymmetry within the number is the analytical point. Spain's services-heavy economy means the exposure runs differently from a manufacturing-heavy country. Manual labour in construction, hospitality service, and agriculture is structurally protected by physical presence. The exposure concentrates in administrative and analytical roles: marketing, mid-office finance, paralegal work, customer support, technical writing, and the administrative spine of professional-services back offices. This inverts the popular story about AI displacement. It is not the obvious blue-collar sectors at immediate risk. It is the white-collar mid-market.

For Spanish SMEs specifically, the implication is acute. A thirty-person hospitality business typically runs three administrative staff and two marketing staff with no internal redeployment runway. When AI augments those roles significantly, the question is not whether the firm benefits — it is whether the people in those seats have been retrained to supervise the tools or simply made redundant. The Spanish government approved a 37,000-position public employment offer on the same day Funcas published the study, carving out dedicated AI and automation roles inside the civil service. [Spain is also building regulatory infrastructure to govern the transition](disclaimer.md), but the private-sector displacement clock is running independently of the legislative pace.
