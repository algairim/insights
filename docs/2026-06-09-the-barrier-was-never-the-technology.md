`2026 June 9`

Ask why an AI project failed and the honest answer is almost never that the technology could not do the job. It is that [the people side was treated as an afterthought](disclaimer.md). The model worked. Nobody changed how the work moved around it, nobody was given a reason to trust it, and within weeks the old habit reasserted itself.

The gap underneath this is literacy, not capability. There is [a wide distance between what AI can actually do and what most employees believe it can do](disclaimer.md), and that distance produces two failure modes at once — staff who refuse a tool that would help them, and staff who quietly adopt tools nobody vetted. The second is already visible everywhere as [shadow AI: tools brought in without IT knowing, accumulating risk in the dark](disclaimer.md). When the biggest IT firms in India [put three hundred thousand staff on the same assistant at once](disclaimer.md), the licence was the easy decision. Whether those people change how they work is the entire question.

The temptation is to buy the tool and call it transformation. The tool is the cheapest part. What separates the firms that get a return from the ones that get a subscription is whether the humans understood it, trusted it, and rebuilt the work around it. That was always the harder thing. It still is.
