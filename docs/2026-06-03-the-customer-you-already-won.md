`2026 June 3`

Across an entire range of small businesses, the same quiet loss repeats: the customer who was happy, who would happily return, and who is never asked. A dental practice tracks who is due a check-up on paper, so the ones who drift away drift away unnoticed. A [med spa sells a treatment built to fade](disclaimer.md) and has nobody watching the calendar for when it does. A [family dental practice with two chairs](disclaimer.md) keeps no shared list of who stopped coming. A [garage where every car returns once a year](disclaimer.md) leaves the reminder to the government. A [physio clinic's recall list lives in someone's memory](disclaimer.md).

In every case the business spends real money to win a customer once, then lets the relationship lapse because nobody owns the follow-up. The work of noticing who is due, who has gone quiet, and who is about to leave is exactly the work [churn prediction and automated recall](disclaimer.md) does without tiring. It is not glamorous and it does not require a frontier model. It requires a system that remembers what the owner used to hold in their head before the business got too big to hold it.

The marketing budget points outward, at strangers. The cheapest growth is sitting in the records, already sold once, waiting to be asked again.
