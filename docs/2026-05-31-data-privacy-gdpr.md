# Data Privacy and AI

`2026 May 31`

Deploying AI on personal data is not a privacy-neutral act. [Under UK GDPR and the EU GDPR](disclaimer.md), any processing of personal data — including feeding customer records, employee data, or communications to an AI model — requires a lawful basis, a legitimate purpose, and appropriate technical and organisational controls. Connecting a client's CRM to a language model API facilitates the processing of personal data and, depending on the arrangement, may create data processor obligations. That means: a data processing agreement must exist, the model provider's data handling terms must be reviewed, and the client must be informed. These are not bureaucratic formalities. They are the foundation of a defensible deployment. [The ICO in the UK has been building statutory AI oversight powers throughout 2026](disclaimer.md), with a statutory AI Code in progress. [Spain's AEPD published its second guidance specifically on AI voice transcription tools in April 2026](disclaimer.md), a sign that regulators are extending data protection requirements into specific AI tool categories. [Canada's privacy commissioners ruled OpenAI violated PIPEDA](disclaimer.md) through AI-generated false personal information.

The practical obligations are not onerous but are easy to neglect under delivery pressure. Maintaining a Record of Processing Activities for AI deployments — which personal data is processed, on which model, for which purpose, with what retention period — is both a legal requirement and a business differentiator. Any organisation that can show a documented AI governance trail is in a stronger position than one that cannot explain what happened to client data. The reputational and contractual exposure for failing to handle personal data correctly is substantially larger than the cost of documenting it.
