`2026 June 3`

Efficiency and resilience pull against each other, and AI is very good at one of them.

A business under cost pressure optimises. It trims the buffer stock, tightens the supplier list to the cheapest option, removes the slack that looked like waste. The [reserve](disclaimer.md) concept names what disappears in that process: the spare capacity that does nothing in normal times and is the only thing standing between a disruption and a failure. The [just-in-time mind](disclaimer.md) concept extends it — a system tuned for maximum efficiency has no give, and the day reality deviates from the plan, there is nothing to absorb it.

What AI changes is that resilience no longer has to mean carrying expensive idle stock. [Continuous supply-chain risk monitoring](disclaimer.md) watches multiple tiers of suppliers and flags the failure before it lands on your desk, and tools that compress [freight and customs paperwork](disclaimer.md) shorten the time between spotting a problem and rerouting around it. Visibility becomes a substitute for buffer. You can run lean and still see the shock coming.

The trap is using the same tools only to cut deeper, optimising away the last of the slack because now you think you can see everything. You can't. Visibility narrows the surprises; it does not abolish them. The reserve was never inefficiency. It was the premium on a risk that hadn't happened yet.
