`2026 June 6`

Choosing an AI vendor feels like buying a tool. It is closer to choosing a landlord. The [study of vendor lock-in](disclaimer.md) maps the dependencies that are hard to leave once they set: prompts tuned to one model's quirks, data formatted for one provider's pipeline, workflows built around one company's pricing. None of it is visible on day one. All of it raises the cost of leaving on the day the price changes or the service degrades.

The [study of the build-versus-buy decision](disclaimer.md) adds the trap underneath: first-year cost lies about the real shape of the commitment, because the expensive part of buying is not the subscription — it is the optionality you give up. And the [study of data residency](disclaimer.md) names a dependency that is now also a legal one: where your prompts physically travel is not just a performance question, it is a compliance question.

The counterweight has arrived faster than expected. [Open-weight models reached the frontier](2026-06-02-open-weights-frontier.md), and the [releases keep coming](disclaimer.md) — [strong models under permissive licences](disclaimer.md) that a business can run on its own terms. The point is not that every firm should self-host. It is that optionality is now a real choice rather than a fantasy, and the time to preserve it is while you are designing the system, not after the bill arrives.
