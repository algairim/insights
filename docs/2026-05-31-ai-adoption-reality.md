`2026 May 31`

Almost every large organisation is running AI in some form. Almost none has made it work at scale. IBM's Think 2026 research found that [only 16% of enterprise AI has scaled beyond pilots](disclaimer.md). A Writer survey of 2,026 executives found 97% reporting some benefit from AI and only 29% seeing significant returns from generative AI specifically. The pattern is consistent across every dataset collected through 2026: almost everyone is getting a little value, almost no one is getting a lot, and the difference is not the technology.

The IBM data names the mechanism precisely. The organisations that do scale are not the ones with better models or bigger budgets. They are the ones that redesigned the work around the AI — defined who owns the outcome, wired in the data, and restructured the roles of the people supervising the systems. The organisations still stuck in pilots skipped that work and assumed the model would handle it. [MIT's GenAI Divide research corroborates this](disclaimer.md): over 95% of enterprise generative AI pilots delivered little or no measurable return, and fewer than 5% moved anything custom into production.

The AI gap is not a tool gap. Every business can access the same models. The gap is an operating model gap — the unglamorous work of deciding which specific process to automate, redesigning the roles around it, and measuring whether it changed anything. Until four questions are answered — what are we automating, who owns the result, where does the data come from, and how do we know it worked — a tool subscription is an expense, not a strategy.
