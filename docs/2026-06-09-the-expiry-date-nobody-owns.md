Some of the most expensive failures in a small business are not dramatic. They are dates that passed while nobody was looking. An electrical contractor [issues certificates that all expire on their own clock, and the reminder to renew belongs to no one in particular](disclaimer.md). A plant-hire firm carries [assets whose compliance lives in a folder, idle past their hire date, earning nothing](disclaimer.md). A crewing operation runs ships where [every certificate expires and the vessel sails short the day one lapses unseen](disclaimer.md). A garage depends on a yearly return where [the only reminder that exists belongs to the government, not the business](disclaimer.md).

The pattern is the same every time. The obligation is known, the date is knowable, and yet it sits in a head, a drawer, or a spreadsheet that nobody is paid to watch. A letting agency with three hundred properties feels this as [a calendar full of statutory dates and six people to hold them](disclaimer.md).

This is the easiest kind of work to automate, because there is nothing fuzzy about it. [Tools that already automate evidence collection and renewal tracking](disclaimer.md) turn a recurring expiry into a scheduled prompt with a name attached. The skill is not the value here. The clock is. And the clock never forgets — only the people do.
