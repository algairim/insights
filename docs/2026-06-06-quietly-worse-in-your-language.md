`2026 June 6`

The large models present the same confident surface in every language. Underneath, the [study of multilingual and low-resource models](disclaimer.md) documents a gap most users never notice: a model that is genuinely strong in English is quietly weaker in languages with less training data — more prone to subtle error, blander phrasing, the occasional confident mistranslation. It sounds fluent, which is exactly what makes the weakness hard to catch.

This matters for any business operating outside the English-speaking core, and it explains the wave of national language models — [Chile's open Spanish-and-Portuguese model](disclaimer.md), [Egypt's Arabic model](disclaimer.md) — built precisely because the frontier was not serving the language well enough. The [study of machine translation](disclaimer.md) draws the practical line: translation at scale is now cheap, but the places where a human still has to read it are exactly the places where a quiet error is expensive — a contract, a medical note, a brand's public voice.

The deeper loss is harder to invoice. The [unlearned language](disclaimer.md) concept observes that a translation delivers the message but withholds the self a language builds — the register, the warmth, the local idiom that signals you belong. A business running its customer communication through a model in a language it cannot itself check is trusting fluency it has no way to verify. The fix is not to avoid the tool. It is to keep a fluent human on the outputs that carry weight.
