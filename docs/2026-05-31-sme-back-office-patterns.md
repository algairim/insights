`2026 May 31`

Research across over sixty SMEs spanning the UK, Spain, and Southeast Asia — spanning ceramics, seafood, care homes, pharmacies, knitwear mills, whisky tourism, olive oil cooperatives, embroidery workshops, and precision engineering — surfaces a pattern that holds with remarkable consistency across sectors and geographies. The variety of automation gaps is minimal despite the diversity of businesses. The same structural problem recurs in almost every case: a process that involves coordinating multiple parties, tracking perishable state — stock, appointments, compliance deadlines, seasonal windows — and routing exceptions, all managed manually, usually by one person, often in a notebook or a spreadsheet. [A letting agency](disclaimer.md) manages 300 properties and a calendar of statutory compliance dates by hand. [A Bigastro citrus cooperative](disclaimer.md) sells fruit to Europe from a website that takes no orders, while 200 growers deliver produce that changes grade by the day. [A Harris Tweed weaver network](disclaimer.md) coordinates 173 home weavers with protected cloth and no shared order view. Sector, country, and size vary. The friction pattern does not.

The pattern's value is its transferability. Someone who recognises the recurring structure — coordinating multi-party state against a perishable constraint, with manual exception handling — can identify it in a food producer in Murcia as readily as in a veterinary clinic in a different sector. Sector knowledge accelerates the engagement; it does not define the underlying diagnosis. [Demand forecasting](disclaimer.md), [field service dispatch](disclaimer.md), [appointment scheduling automation](disclaimer.md), and [invoice automation](disclaimer.md) each address one variant of the same pattern.
