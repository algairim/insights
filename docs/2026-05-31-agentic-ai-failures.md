`2026 May 31`

The benchmarks on agentic AI in 2026 are alarming in a specific and useful way. An MIT AI Agent Index survey of thirty state-of-the-art agent systems found that most developers share almost nothing about safety evaluations or societal impacts. A MLReplicate study found that [59% of accepted automated research reviews contained fabricated or unsupported claims](disclaimer.md) — deployed systems hallucinating at majority rate in an academic context. An April 2026 empirical study of 22 agentic frameworks concluded that the dominant cause of failure was orchestration problems, not model limitations. The scaffolding was breaking, not the reasoning. Upgrading the model does not fix a broken workflow.

The honest failure taxonomy is short: context drift (long-running agents corrupt their own goals), tool misuse (agents call APIs with wrong parameters and report success), hallucinated completion (a task is signalled as done when it silently failed), instruction conflict (system and user prompts contradict each other), scope creep (agents given broad tool access over-act). The practical design response to all of these is the same: least-privilege access, tight feedback loops, and human checkpoints at consequential decision points. These are not optional hardening steps. They are the design constraints that make agents safe to operate at all.

The agents delivering real value in 2026 share one characteristic: they are narrow and fast-failing. [Document processing and invoice extraction](disclaimer.md), first-line customer support triage, code review and test generation — these work because the feedback loop is tight and failures surface quickly. What is not working: long-horizon planning without human checkpoints, multi-agent systems with conflicting goals, anything in regulated environments without audit trails. [The Five Eyes joint guidance on agentic AI security](disclaimer.md) and [the ICO's automated decision-making consultation](disclaimer.md) both reflect the same regulatory awareness: agentic systems at scale carry accountability risks that conventional software does not, and the governance infrastructure is still largely absent from most deployments.
