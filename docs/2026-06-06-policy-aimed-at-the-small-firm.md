`2026 June 6`

For most of the AI policy era, the small firm was an afterthought — the rules were written for frontier labs and large enterprises, and the corner shop was left to work it out alone. That is changing, and a business owner who follows only the headlines will miss it. [Europe's sovereignty package quietly lowered the AI Act compliance bar for small firms](disclaimer.md). [Singapore aimed its AI strategy explicitly at the corner shop, not the headline enterprise](disclaimer.md). [Italy signed an AI factory into being whose real intended customer is the SME](disclaimer.md).

The support is increasingly financial and direct, not just rhetorical. [Spain put AI inside its digitalisation grant catalogue and pays SMEs to adopt it](disclaimer.md); [a major lab brought a structured SME accelerator to small firms](disclaimer.md). The money and the programmes exist. What is missing is rarely the funding — it is a small firm with the time and the confidence to find the right scheme and act on it.

This is the gap that the [AI consulting opportunity](2026-05-31-ai-consulting-opportunity.md) insight identified from the other side: a quarter of SME owners want external help and few have received it. The two facts fit together. There has never been more public support aimed at the small firm adopting AI, and there has rarely been less capacity inside those firms to use it. The opportunity is not the technology. It is the translation between the two.
