`2026 June 9`

Every answer a model gives has a physical cost that nobody puts on the invoice. [The energy, water, and carbon spent on AI queries run in the background while the user sees only a result](disclaimer.md). At the scale of one prompt it is invisible. At the scale of an industry it is rewriting where the world builds, and who pays for the power.

The map of new data centres is really a map of where electricity is cheap and abundant. [Paraguay's hydropower stopped mining bitcoin and started running AI](disclaimer.md); [Microsoft underwrote South Africa's grid as the precondition for AI before it built anything](disclaimer.md); [Brazil is pre-leasing AI-native data centres before the walls go up](disclaimer.md). There is also a counter-movement worth noticing: [Africa's AI bet may turn out to be small and local rather than the gigawatt race](disclaimer.md), closer to where the demand actually is.

For a business none of this shows up directly, and that is the point. The cost is real, it is rising, and it is paid by someone downstream — a grid, a water table, a community near a substation. As usage-based pricing ties a firm's AI bill ever more tightly to how much it runs, the energy underneath the answer stops being an externality and starts becoming a number. Worth knowing it is there before it arrives.
