`2026 June 9`

The better an automated system gets, the less its human supervisor practises stepping in. The [Deference Reflex](disclaimer.md) concept names the result: the skill to override atrophies precisely on the day it is finally needed. This is the quiet cost of reliability. A model that is right ninety-nine times trains the person watching it to stop checking, so that the hundredth time, when it is confidently wrong, nobody is paying enough attention to catch it.

It is not a hypothetical. A large company that pushed AI to the front of its customer service [had to rehire humans after the push backfired](disclaimer.md). The economics make the trap worse — the [Verification Tax](disclaimer.md) concept captures it: generation has become nearly free while checking the output stays expensive, so the pressure is always to verify less. But the cost of a missed error does not fall just because the tool got cheaper — and [when the AI is wrong, the policies on who actually pays are mostly silent](disclaimer.md).

The discipline that keeps this honest is deciding in advance [where a human stays in the loop, and where removing them costs more than it saves](disclaimer.md). The point is not to distrust the tool. It is to keep the override warm — to keep one foot on the ground for the moment the confident answer is the wrong one, because that moment is the only one that was ever going to matter.
